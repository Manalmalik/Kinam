The Legend of the Creation of Man
Many ages ago, when the only things that existed were the calm, quiet sea, the sky and “Huracán”, the almighty collective Spiritual Energy making up the heart of the sky, twins Tepeu and Gucumatz came during the darkness of night. The creator and the “shaper” were meditating on the sea. They broke the silence with the spoken word and in their state of complete clarity, they decided to create Man by joining their words and thoughts. Huracán declared he would manifest life into the trees, plants and land. Everything that would help sustain this new creation of life occurred in their thoughts and became reality. They agreed to create Dawn to allow the formation of life, and thus made day and light.

The First Creation (animals)
The creators made deer, birds, lions, tigers, snakes, and vipers to be the guardians of nature, protecting forests, tree branches, mountains, and valleys. The creators called for them, asking them to call their names, but the animals could not speak besides making noises. The animals were then condemned to guard the lands forever and be the meat for others.

The Second Creation (first humans)
The twins first tried creating humans out of mud, but their creations were blind, had no senses and couldn’t speak a comprehensible language. Soon enough, they fell apart and their faces melted off. Then they tried using wood to create Man. This was more successful, but their creations possessed no soul; they were mindless robots, meaning that they soon forgot about their makers and stopped worshipping them. Huracán set out to destroy the new race with powerful rain and storms, causing the animals to turn against the wooden people. Even their pots and querns rebelled and crushed the peoples' faces. What was left of the wooden people escaped to the forests and soon were turned into monkeys.

The Final Creation: The Maize Men
Tepeu & Gucumatz joined forces one last time to experiment with corn in attempts to create Man. They formed a man made of masa, or corn dough, who was able to speak and had a strong soul. Their creations could see for miles wherever they turned, so the gods formed mountains for humans to climb and see as far as their eyes could reach. They also made rivers to allow Man to travel and feed their fields. Since then, the humans give thanks to the gods for giving them these gifts, and they set out to explore every corner of the world.

The Legend of Tecun Uman & The Quetzal
This is the story of Tecun Uman, the last Prince, Commander and Defender of the K’iche Maya Kingdom of the highlands of Guatemala, and his spirit guardian, El Quetzal. In Maya belief, it is said that every human being comes into this world with an energy that guides and protects its existence. This guardian spirit has a physical representation on Earth in the shape of an animal, called “Nawal”. Tecun Uman’s Nawal was “el quetzal”, a small, beautiful bird with a long green, blue tail and stained red chest. It has been flying through the rainforests of Mesoamerica for thousands of years, and tales and recordings of it are still conserved in Maya writings.

During the 1500’s, when the Spaniards, commanded by Hernan Cortes, were settled in Mexican lands and had already become allies with the local rulers, they heard that the powerful Maya kingdom of the Kiche had denied their loyalty to the Spanish crown and refused to allow the Spaniards to settle in Guatemalan lands. Cortés decided to dispatch Pedro de Alvarado with 180 cavalry, 300 infantry, crossbows, muskets, 4 cannons, large amounts of ammunition and gunpowder, and thousands of allied Mexican warriors from Tlaxcala, Cholula and other cities in central Mexico. When Prince Tecun Uman saw that his people were threatened by the foreign invasion, he guided their troops into battle with courage and dignity, to protect his people from the Spanish conquistadors.

The legends say that Tecun Uman entered into battle adorned with precious quetzal feathers, and his Nawal quetzal bird, accompanied him in the fight. In the midst of the battle, in the valleys of Xelaju, Alvarado and Tecun Uman met face to face, each with weapon in hand. Alvarado was clad in armor and mounted on his warhorse. As horses were not native to the Americas, and Mesoamerican peoples had no beasts of their own, Tecun Uman assumed they made up one being, and he killed Alvarado's horse. After falling from his horse, the rider pierced his steel sword through Tecun Uman and hit his heart. Filled with grief, the K'iche' prince's Nawal landed on the fallen hero's chest, staining its breast feathers red with blood and died thereafter. From that day, all male quetzals bear a scarlet breast and their song has not been heard since. Also, if a quetzal is placed in captivity, it will die, making the quetzal a symbol of liberty.

The Legend of Ixchel & Itzamná
Legend has it, in the days when gods were still mortal, there existed a love so deep and pure that its strength created the sun and moon, the day and night and the shining stars. A long time ago, in a land far away, there lived a beautiful woman named Ixchel, whose beauty entranced many men across the lands, including our young hero Itzamná. The charming Itzamná began to dazzle the coveted Ixchel, and deep love began to grow in both of them. Their destiny was written in the skies.

One sunny day in paradise, a young stranger arrived in the village. Upon seeing Ixchel, he was instantly smitten and proclaimed his love for the young woman. Unaware of the growing love between Ixchel and Itzamná, Ixchel’s sister, Ixtab, organized a fight to the death between the two men for the love of the maiden. On the day of the big fight, Itzamná was destined to become the triumphant winner. Unfortunately, fate did not account for a cheating move from his opponent, wounding Itzamná in the back and causing him to drop dead.

After seeing her beloved dead, Ixchel ran to him and took her own life. She passed her soul to her sister Ixtab, who became the goddess of suicide. Ixtap cursed the warrior and his dirty tricks, making him disappear without a trace, never to be spoken of again. The two young lovers traveled to the heavens to celebrate their love for the rest of the time. Itzamná was reborn as the sun god and Ixchel became the goddess of the moon, she is also considered the goddess of childbirth and fertility. To celebrate eternal love for his beloved, Itzamná gave Ixchel the brightness of the night in the form of stars. The legend says that every soul who dies of love becomes a star, shining their love for eternity.
