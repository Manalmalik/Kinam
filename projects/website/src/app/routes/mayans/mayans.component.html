Having constructed complex cities in the deepest and sheer unreachable parts of the jungle, the ancient Mayas are until today one of the most fascinating and still undiscovered civilizations in human history. The Mayan civilization developed in an area that encompasses all of Guatemala and Belize, southeastern Mexico, and the western portions of Honduras and El Salvador. The first Maya cities developed around 750 BC, and by 500 BC these cities possessed monumental architecture, including large temples with elaborate façades. Their legacy can be seen in numerous other aspects of daily life, such as in astronomy, mathematics, farming, language, and art. They achieved great progress in many fields and were sometimes centuries ahead of Western civilizations in some of their discoveries. For instance, the Mayas created one of the first-ever recorded concepts of zero and developed a unique numbering system using only dots and lines, which was more advanced than any other number system at that time. They also invented a highly sophisticated writing system composed of beautiful symbols known as Maya glyphs. The most iconic symbol remaining today are the impressive temples in the jungle, built on pyramids and dedicated to their gods. It’s thanks to the Mayas that we eat corn, beans, squash, chili pepper, amaranth, and many other staple crops. Their farming methods were both sustainable and highly functional, only hunting and planting the necessary amount to feed their large and diverse populations. Caring for the community, rather than the individual, was always of utmost importance to them. The Mayas were very ceremonial people and believed in the supernatural. They performed rituals to worship the gods and their ancestors to bring them goodwill. Above all, the Mayas believe in respect and love for all life, cultures and traditions. They strive for a symbiotic relationship with nature and seek to live harmoniously with everything around them. In 2017, Maya archeologists and technology experts from Guatemala and the United States mounted advanced laser scanners onto helicopters to create the biggest ever 3D survey of the jungle in order to find out how much of the ancient civilization is still hidden underneath the jungle canopy today. The results were shocking: The laser scans uncovered the ruins of more than 60,000 structures that could be entire cities, houses, palaces, elevated highways, and other human-made features - an incredible amount of scientific knowledge and cultural beauty which is now waiting to be discovered and shared with the world.
